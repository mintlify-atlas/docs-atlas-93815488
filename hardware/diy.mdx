Build your own controller adapter with a microcontroller board, a few components, and soldering iron. This page covers what you need, where to buy it, and guidance for different build types.

## What you need

<Steps>
  <Step title="Choose a microcontroller board">
    Pick a board based on your use case. The **Adafruit KB2040** is the default recommendation for console adapters. See [Supported Boards](/hardware/boards) for the full list.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Get a USB cable">
    USB-C or Micro-USB depending on your board. Must be a data-capable cable — charge-only cables have no data lines and will not work.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Get the console connector">
    Each console uses a different connector. Cut a controller extension cable to get the correct plug, or source a raw connector. See the [Wiring Guide](/hardware/wiring) for pinouts.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Get a USB-A breakout (most builds)">
    Most builds need a USB-A female breakout board to accept USB controllers. The Feather RP2040 USB Host skips this step — it has a built-in USB-A port.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Wire and solder">
    Connect board GPIO pins to the console connector and USB-A breakout using 22–26 AWG hookup wire. See the [Wiring Guide](/hardware/wiring) for pin assignments and diagrams.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Flash the firmware">
    Download the correct `.uf2` from [Releases](https://github.com/joypad-ai/joypad-os/releases) or build from source. Hold BOOT while connecting USB to enter bootloader mode, then drag and drop the file.
  </Step>
</Steps>

**Full parts list:**
- Microcontroller board (see [Supported Boards](/hardware/boards))
- USB cable (data-capable, USB-C or Micro-USB)
- Console connector or extension cable (cut to expose wires)
- USB-A female breakout board or cut USB-A extension cable
- 22–26 AWG hookup wire
- Soldering iron and solder

Optional: level shifters, pullup resistors, capacitors (console-specific — see individual adapter docs).

## Build types

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Console adapter">
    **Use any USB or Bluetooth controller on a retro console.**

    Example: `usb2gc_kb2040` — USB controllers to GameCube

    **What you need:**
    - Adafruit KB2040 (~$10)
    - GameCube controller extension cable (cut)
    - USB-A female breakout board
    - Hookup wire, soldering iron

    Wire the GameCube plug to GPIO 7 (data) and the USB-A breakout to GPIO 16/17. Flash the firmware for your target console. Full wiring diagram in the [usb2gc build guide](/hardware/wiring).

    **Supported output consoles**: GameCube/Wii, PCEngine/TurboGrafx-16, Dreamcast, Nuon, 3DO, Neo Geo, Casio Loopy
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="USB passthrough">
    **Bridge USB controllers to USB output — for remapping, protocol conversion, or wireless-to-wired.**

    Example: `usb2usb_feather` — USB/BT controller to USB HID gamepad

    **What you need (easiest option):**
    - Adafruit Feather RP2040 USB Host (~$12) — has built-in USB-A host port, no wiring needed
    - USB-C cable

    Flash `usb2usb` firmware. Plug in a USB controller, plug the Feather into a PC. The controller appears as a USB HID gamepad with full button remapping via profiles.

    For other RP2040 boards, wire a USB-A breakout to GPIO 16/17. See the [Wiring Guide](/hardware/wiring).
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Bluetooth to USB">
    **Convert Bluetooth controllers to wired USB — no soldering required.**

    Example: `bt2usb_pico_w` — Bluetooth controller to USB gamepad

    **What you need:**
    - Raspberry Pi Pico W (~$6)
    - Micro-USB data cable

    That is it. The Pico W has built-in Classic Bluetooth and BLE. Flash the firmware, plug in, and pair your controller. See the [bt2usb Pico W build guide](/hardware/wiring).
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Native controller adapter">
    **Wire a retro controller directly to GPIO pins — no USB involved in the data path.**

    Example: `snes2usb_kb2040` — SNES controller to USB

    **What you need:**
    - Adafruit KB2040 (~$10)
    - SNES, N64, or GameCube extension cable (cut)
    - Hookup wire, soldering iron

    Wire the controller's clock, latch, and data lines to the designated GPIO pins. See the [Wiring Guide](/hardware/wiring) for SNES, N64, and GameCube pinouts.

    **Supported native inputs**: SNES/NES, N64, GameCube, Neo Geo arcade stick, LodgeNet hotel controllers
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Custom controller">
    **Wire up your own buttons, sticks, and sensors to build a USB gamepad from scratch.**

    Example: `controller_macropad` — Adafruit MacroPad to USB HID

    **What you need:**
    - Adafruit MacroPad RP2040 (~$30), or any RP2040 board with GPIO buttons
    - Buttons, switches, or an analog stick module

    Wire buttons to GPIO pins and configure the firmware for your layout. The MacroPad is a self-contained option with 12 mechanical switches and an OLED display already integrated.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Common mistakes

Avoid these before powering on your build:

- **Reversed power polarity** — double-check + and − before connecting
- **Wrong voltage** — most console data lines are 3.3V; applying 5V will damage the board
- **Crossed data lines** — D+ and D- swapped on the USB host port will silently fail
- **Cold solder joints** — resolder any joint that looks dull or grainy
- **Missing pullup resistors** — some console protocols require pullups; check the adapter docs
- **Wrong GPIO pins** — pin assignments differ between board variants; verify before flashing
- **Charge-only USB cable** — must use a data-capable cable to flash and communicate
